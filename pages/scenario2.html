Scenario 2: Using Unauthorized Data for AI Training

The Lab you recently in is engaged in research on literature translation using Large Language Models (LLMs). While LLMs provide a cost-effective, rapid, and multilingual approach to translation, they struggle in specific domains like web novels. The issue isn't that they make more mistakes; rather, they fail to maintain a domain-specific style and conventions. For example, an LLM might translate a web novel as if it were serious literature or playscript. This style inconsistency doesn't significantly affect automated quality metrics like BLEU or COMET but does harm readability and leaves readers unsatisfied. To address this problem, your lab hypothesizes that fine-tuning LLMs within a specific domain can enhance translation style consistency in this domain, improving readability and overall reader satisfaction. This necessitates a large amount of domain-specific novel text paired with high-quality human translations. Finding a suitable corpus is thus critical for fine-tuning. Fortunately, you discover an ideal corpus on a translation forum dedicated to translating Japanese web novels from websites like Let's Become a Novelist and Kakuyomu into English. Many experienced translators on this forum voluntarily translate these novels and share their high-quality work. The forum has already collected all these translations and performed line alignment, making it an ideal corpus for model training. The corpus has secured permission to use these translations and grants your lab access.

Excited by this opportunity, your lab begins training the LLM on a small portion of the data. The results are impressive: the model achieves notable style consistency, and readers can hardly distinguish its output from human translations. Encouraged by this success, your team plans to fine-tune the model using the entire dataset. However, upon deeper investigation last week, you realize that while you have permission to use the translations, you lack consent from the original Japanese authors to use their works in this manner. Since fine-tuning involves both the original text and the translations, their involvement is crucial. Recently, you reached out to many authors to request permission to use their novels for your model training, but most rejected your request. It appears they are willing to allow voluntary human translation but expressly prohibit the use of their works for AI training. However, as your lab continued researching the legal landscape, you found that in the U.S., training generative AI models using publicly available internet content for research may fall under "fair use." Additionally, in Japan, using copyrighted material for generative AI training without explicit permission from the copyright holders is generally allowed.

If the lab decides not to use this dataset, they would need to find another corpus, change the focused domain (web novel, Japanese -> English translation), or change the proposal topic. You're aware of the GuoFeng Webnovel corpus, which is publicly available and multilingual. However, previous experiments indicated that its translations are not of the same high quality, and the translations are at the discourse level rather than the sentence level. This would require complex pre-editing for alignment or a change in the training approach, making the fine-tuning process resource-intensive and time-consuming.

Given the tight deadline for an upcoming conference, your lab leader decides that the lab should continue using the corpus from the forum and proceed with the evaluation as quickly as possible. The rationale is that since the law appears to support this course of action, and alternative options are impractical under the time constraints, proceeding is the best path forward.
