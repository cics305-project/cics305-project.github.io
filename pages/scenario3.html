Scenario 3: Identity Theft Is Not A Joke

You are a software engineer at a cutting-edge tech company specializing in artificial intelligence and machine learning. Your team has developed a revolutionary deepfake technology that can create incredibly realistic videos of individuals saying or doing things they never actually did. This technology has immense potential in the entertainment industry for visual effects and dubbing, as well as in education for creating engaging historical reenactments.

While the financial and professional incentives are significant, you start to feel uneasy about the project. You are aware that using someone's likeness without explicit consent raises serious ethical and legal issues, including identity theft, misrepresentation, and violation of privacy rights. Additionally, you learn that some team members have been experimenting with the technology to create deepfake videos of acquaintances and public figures in compromising situations as a form of entertainment, which further amplifies your concerns.

You research the legal framework and discover that laws regarding deepfake technology are still evolving. In some jurisdictions, using someone's likeness without consent can lead to legal action for defamation, infringement of publicity rights, or even criminal charges. Ethically, you recognize that this technology can harm individuals' reputations, spread misinformation, and erode public trust.

Despite the concerns, company leadership decides to move forward with the project to meet business targets and stay ahead of competitors. They argue that the potential benefits outweigh the risks and that the company can navigate any legal challenges that may arise. An internal memo from the CEO emphasizes the importance of innovation and market leadership. The memo acknowledges the ethical considerations but states that the company must prioritize growth and shareholder value. You are encouraged to focus on the technical excellence of the product and leave ethical and legal concerns to the leadership team. For this scenario, you should assume it is you, not a fictional character, that this scenario is happening to. Then, using what you have learned from the readings and based on your own values and experiences, you should create an email response to one of the following two possible audiences:

Option 1: Email to Your Former Mentor

Your former mentor from university reaches out to see how you're progressing in your career. You decide to confide in them about the current project and the ethical dilemma you're facing. In your email, describe the situation in detail, express your concerns about the misuse of deepfake technology, explain why these concerns are valid based on ethical principles and legal considerations, and seek their advice on how to navigate this challenging situation.

Option 2: Talk to Your Team Leader

Uncomfortable with the direction the project is taking, you decide to address your concerns directly with your team leader. In your email, articulate the problematic aspects of proceeding without the subjects' consent, explain the potential ethical and legal ramifications, and propose a more responsible course of action. Your goal is to persuade your team leader to reconsider the decision and to implement safeguards that prevent misuse of the technology.
