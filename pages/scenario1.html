Scenario 1: AI-Generated Content

Your company provides data information services to content providers who primarily serve customers on the internet. A new client provides content for a suite of lifestyle websites that include cooking, travel, fitness, and health information. The client has consistent ad revenue, mostly from a couple of sponsors that use the site to market their healthcare and lifestyle products. Despite not having financial woes, the client has decided to downsize their content team and wants to replace 40% of their image division with AI-generated content. This division produces and places the images that appear in context with the articles posted by the text division. Your team has been asked to work with the client to set up a process for their website that will feed AI generated images directly to the remaining 3 members of the image division who will moderate and then approve placement of the content.

Your team has not worked directly with AI-generated content before, and your manager is excited by the potential for future client contracts that might be possible. The legal team expressed some concerns about how using AI-generated images might weaken the copyright held for published content, but ultimately your team was given the green light to proceed. Now you are in the design phase of building a rough interface for the image team to use so that they can view and approve the AI-generated images. Meanwhile, you’ve been reading about the diﬀerent image generators available in order to select one to test for this project. You start to develop concerns about the derivative nature of AI generated images, and then you read about how real people employed as moderators and reviewers of generated content have suﬀered as a result. You decide to share your concerns with a team member, but this technology has them completely impressed. They tell you not to worry, that the company has already thought of all these things and that you should let it go and just have fun working on the project. At first you take their advice, until, one of the image team abruptly quits their job after sending you a stern email about not being able to view any more images ‘like those.’ You aren’t sure what exactly caused the problem, but you now feel worried that you should have brought your concerns to management sooner. For this scenario, you should assume it is you, not a fictional character, that this scenario is happening to. Then, using what you have learned from the readings and based on your own values and experiences, you should create an email response to one of the following two possible audiences:

Option 1: Email to a Former Professor

A favorite professor from your previous school reached out to you to see how you are liking the job. You respond by describing the project described in the case, and then you share with your previous instructor what concerns you have related to this project. You should expect to explain the situation, identify your concerns, describe, and explain why those concerns are valid, discuss any questions that you think you need to ask, and then volunteer what you see as the decision you must make moving forward (if any).

Option 2: Talk to Your Manager

You decide that you are uncomfortable carrying out this project without addressing some key questions and concerns, and you decide to talk to your manager about it. Even though the project has is underway, your goal is to try to get the manager to address the ethical questions that have arisen for you and what impacts this project might have for the client, for your company, and potentially the public. You will need to describe the aspects of the project that concern you or raise questions for you, explaining why, and you will need to explain why you think there are ethical issues in this project that need to be resolved.
